1. Historical Context
Matthew begins his Gospel with a genealogy of Jesus, tracing His lineage from Abraham through David and ending with Joseph, husband of Mary.
This genealogy is more than a historical record; it's a theological statement. By linking Jesus to Abraham, Matthew emphasizes Jesus as the fulfillment of the promises
made to Abraham and the Jewish people. The reference to David highlights Jesus' royal lineage, aligning Him with the Jewish expectation of a Messianic king.
This chapter sets the stage against the backdrop of Jewish history, under Roman occupation, where the longing for a Messiah who would deliver Israel from its oppressors was intense.

2. New Testament Theological Context
Matthew's genealogy establishes Jesus' legal right to the throne of David, affirming His identity as the Messiah.
The inclusion of four womenâTamar, Rahab, Ruth, and Bathsheba (referred to as "Uriah's wife")âeach with unconventional stories, underscores the theme of God's grace and
the unexpected ways in which God's salvation history unfolds. Joseph's obedience to God, despite societal expectations, highlights the theme of righteousness that
Matthew will continue to explore throughout his Gospel.

3. Old Testament Theological Context
The genealogy of Jesus as presented in Matthew echoes numerous Old Testament promises and prophecies. It recalls God's covenant with
Abraham (Genesis 12:1-3) and the promise of a king from the line of David (2 Samuel 7:12-16). By situating Jesus within this lineage,
Matthew demonstrates that Jesus is the culmination of God's redemptive plan as revealed throughout the Old Testament.

4. Commentary on Application
Matthew 1 invites readers to reflect on the themes of promise, fulfillment, and unexpected grace.
It challenges us to see beyond our expectations of how God should act, reminding us that God often works through the marginalized and overlooked to fulfill His purposes.
The obedience of Joseph, despite personal cost, serves as a model of faithfulness and trust in God's plan.
